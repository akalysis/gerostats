Unpaid care across Tyne and Wear

9 June 2026

The hidden week being carried in plain sight.

Across Newcastle, Gateshead, North Tyneside, South Tyneside and Sunderland, Census 2021 shows 24,107 people aged 65 and over providing unpaid care across Tyne and Wear. On a cautious lower-bound count, that is at least 726,492 care-hours every week. It is older people getting someone washed, dressed, fed, medicated, watched over, taken to appointments, settled at night, and held together again the next day.

Some places are not just poorer. They are doing more care, with less money, poorer health, and less margin for anything to go wrong.

What this shows

Unpaid care is not evenly shared.

The map is not saying that care is a problem. Care is love, duty, reciprocity, family, friendship and survival. The issue is intersectionality: older age, unpaid care, poor health, disability and deprivation are not separate lines on a table. In some wards, they intersect in the same lives and streets.

In the most deprived fifth of local neighbourhoods here, the minimum older-carer burden is about 418 hours per 100 residents aged 65 and over each week. In the lowest older-care burden fifth, it is about 246 hours. That is not a small difference in a week. It is a different social load.

Denton & Westerhope makes this personal.

Denton & Westerhope is where I grew up, so it should not disappear behind a top-five ranking. It is not the highest ward by rate per 100 residents aged 65 and over, but it carries a very large amount of the hidden week: at least 10,320 unpaid care-hours from older residents every week.

In this map it ranks second across Tyne and Wear for total older-care hours, and second for the number of residents aged 65 and over reporting 50 or more hours of unpaid care a week. That is the point of making the map personal: the places that formed us can also be places where public statistics become harder to keep at a distance.

Imagine if this stopped tomorrow.

Thousands of older people are doing work the formal care system would otherwise have to see, fund and staff. If that unpaid labour stopped tomorrow, the pressure would not disappear. It would move into GP surgeries, hospitals, social care waiting lists, family finances and the health of the carers themselves.

That is the take-home: when older carers are also in poorer, more disabled and more deprived places, resilience is not an endless resource. It is infrastructure, even when it happens in kitchens, bedrooms, buses, clinics, pharmacies and broken sleep.

Take-home

If the hidden labour stopped.

The policy question is not only how many hours are being provided. It is who is providing them, where, and with what margin left. If older unpaid carers in high-burden places became too ill, too poor, or too exhausted to continue, the hidden week would become a visible service crisis very quickly.
