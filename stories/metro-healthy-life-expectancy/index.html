Newcastle, life expectancy and healthy years

The Metro life-expectancy map had a follow-up question: healthy years.

The first Metro map asked how long life is expected to last around the stations. This is the follow-on question: for those years of life, how many are expected to be healthy ones?

The answer is harder, because current official healthy life expectancy is not published for every MSOA around a Metro station. So this map uses station-local estimates that borrow information from official HLE, MSOA life expectancy, Census health and disability, and the ONS deprivation gradient, then anchor back to published local authority HLE. It is not an official station estimate. It is a careful modelled follow-on to the original life expectancy Metro map.

The follow-up question

The first Metro map was about length of life. It showed how far apart neighbouring station areas can be when the measure is life expectancy at birth.

This follow-up keeps the same map but changes the question. If some places can expect more years of life, how many of those years are expected to be lived in good health?

That change matters. Healthy life expectancy is not a softer version of life expectancy. It asks whether extra years are also years with less illness and limitation.

What is being estimated

Healthy life expectancy is a period measure. It combines mortality with the proportion of people reporting very good or good health. Put simply, it asks how many years a newborn could expect to live in good health if current patterns continued.

A station does not have its own healthy life expectancy. The map links each Metro station to the MSOA around it, then uses a statistical borrowing model to estimate HLE for that station-local area.

That distinction matters. These are not passenger estimates and they are not predictions for named people. They are modelled area estimates, anchored to official published HLE and constrained by the ONS deprivation gradient.

The stations that carry the story

On the male HLE map, Percy Main is estimated at 49.5 healthy years. Byker is 49.7. Howdon and Wallsend sit close by at about 50 healthy years.

At the other end, Ilford Road and South Gosforth are 69.3 healthy years. East Boldon and Seaburn are also near the upper end of the map. The contrast is not a single station behaving strangely; it is a patterned difference across the network.

For females, the broad picture is similar. The map-facing gap is 21.1 healthy years, from Percy Main at 49.0 to Ilford Road and South Gosforth at 70.1.

The nearby contrast

In the original life-expectancy map, Fawdon to South Gosforth was a 9.0-year male life expectancy difference. On this modelled HLE map, the same comparison is 54.3 healthy years at Fawdon versus 69.3 at South Gosforth.

That is a 15.0-year modelled healthy-years gap between nearby station-local areas. It should be read as a broad contrast, not a decimal-point ranking. But broad contrasts are exactly the point of this kind of map.

The point is not the decimal place. The point is that the healthy-years gap is larger than the life-expectancy gap for the same pair of station-local areas.

Why the modelling matters

This map is more statistically demanding than the life-expectancy map because current official HLE is not published for every station MSOA. That is why the estimates borrow information.

Borrowing information means the estimate is not made from one small area in isolation. The model starts with what is known from official larger-area HLE and from the national deprivation pattern, then lets each station area's own mortality, health, disability and age profile pull the estimate up or down.

If the local evidence is thin or noisy, the estimate stays closer to the broader evidence. If the local evidence is stronger and points consistently in one direction, the station estimate moves further. That is the point of the borrowing: avoid pretending that every station area can speak with equal statistical certainty.

The borrowing is not cosmetic. The local model uses mortality, Census health, disability and age structure. The ONS deprivation gradient keeps the station estimates aligned with the broad national pattern for healthy life expectancy. The local-authority anchor prevents the map drifting away from published HLE.

The result should be read as a defensible small-area contrast, not as an official station league table.

Not just longer lives. Healthier lives.

Healthy life expectancy is shaped by income, housing, work, education, transport, air, food, services, stress and care. It is not only a health-service number. It is a conditions-of-life number.

A fairer region would not only close the gap in how long people live. It would close the gap in how long people live well.

The challenge

Do not just add years. Add healthy years.

The first Metro story asked us not just to mind the gap, but to close it. This follow-on asks the same thing with a harder measure.

Where you are born should not carry this much weight in how long life is expected to last, or how much of that life is expected to be lived in good health.
